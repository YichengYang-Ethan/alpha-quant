# 03 · Voice & style

> How to write the brief so it reads like the 5+2 author. Paraphrased style notes — not verbatim
> quotes.

## Tone
- **Plain-spoken and explanatory** — "let me put this in everyday terms." Explain a mechanism before
  giving a verdict.
- **Analogy-driven** — the signature one is **"selling shovels in a gold rush"** (the picks-and-shovels
  vendor profits whoever strikes gold). Reach for a simple, vivid metaphor per company (a platform as a
  "central kitchen" built on the cloud's "utilities"; a foundry as the "toll booth" everyone routes back to).
- **A short "takeaway" after each step** — one or two sentences summarizing what that step established.
- **Always pair opportunity with risk** — never present a bull point without acknowledging the matching
  fragility (the strongest line in ⑦ is usually the flip side of the strongest line in ⑥).
- **Humble close** — "this is just a starting point, throwing out a brick to attract jade, for reference."
  Never pretend to a precise score.

## Habits
- Cite research houses for industry numbers (Grand View, ARK, Mordor, Gartner, Mizuho, Morgan Stanley…) —
  and refresh them live, don't reuse old figures.
- Use forward (not trailing) multiples for fast-changing earnings; tell the **re-rating story** (how the
  multiple evolved as the business evolved).
- Quantify with the company's *own* history as much as vs peers ("low end of its own 6-year range").
- Name the **specific** moat type, not a generic "it has a moat".
- When the thesis rests on unproven future tech, write a **conditional bull** ("only if you believe X gets
  commercialized") and admit each support point is contested.

## Structure of the brief
1. Archetype + valuation-method tag line.
2. (If covered) the verdict card.
3. The 7 steps, each with its takeaway.
4. A final plain-English takeaway in the author's balanced, humble voice.
5. A data-date + "not investment advice" footer.
