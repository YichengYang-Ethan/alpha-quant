# 5+2 Rules (distilled from the method) — read AFTER the archetype router

> 🧭 **Read `references/06-archetype-router.md` first**: for *any* ticker, classify the archetype →
> take the matching playbook → *then* come here for the case-bound detail. **The `[TICKER]` tags
> below are examples, not hard rules**; the general rule is "classify → apply playbook → fall back
> to the universal playbook if nothing matches".
> **Structure**: 5+2 = 7 steps, 5 objective + 2 subjective.

## ⭐ Calibration meta-rules (highest priority — cut across every step)
1. **Always refresh the TAM live** — pull market size/CAGR from a named research firm (Grand View/
   Mordor/Gartner), latest figure, correct *narrow* market definition. Never reuse stale numbers or
   hand-wave "hundreds of billions".
2. **Calibrate the valuation conclusion; don't err to either side** — anchor the **DCF base BELOW the
   analyst mean** (he runs conservative); judge cheap/expensive with a **quality-adjusted peer comp**
   (the Meritech 20%-growth + 20%-FCF rule), not against a single rival; **tone: contextualize, don't
   catastrophize**.
3. **Judge management by track record** — drop any mechanical "non-founder = question mark" penalty;
   a professional CEO is judged on actual execution. **But don't swing all-positive either** — keep the
   critical nuance (a CEO can be pragmatic yet slow on a key trend; "is this the CEO or the founder
   behind the scenes pulling the strings?").
4. **⑦ must include the "paradigm disruption" layer, and don't pile on generic risks** — beyond
   competition/valuation, always ask "could the whole business model be bypassed by a new paradigm"
   (AI agents reading raw data → the middle layer is skipped). Don't list risks the company has already
   neutralized, and **don't substitute generic competition/valuation risks for the *specific* ones he
   names** (e.g. cash-flow + succession).
5. **Rating card behind a coverage gate** — the free write-up is the understanding layer with no card;
   only a covered name emits a rating/tier/target. **Target ∈ [DCF base, bull]** (can sit above base on
   conviction).

### ① Industry
- One-line positioning → pin the core trend → **TAM + growth (cite research)** → adoption → structure +
  key customers → industry-level risk. Tell the industry/company history to see the structure.
- **Industry growth ≠ the company winning** — even in a tailwind, check the company's *share/competitive
  trend* (a carmaker can lose share while the EV market grows). But a growing pie can offset share loss
  (ad share can fall while the pie grows faster → absolute revenue still rises).
- **Cyclicality = structural valuation discount** — strong-cyclical/economy-sensitive businesses (banks,
  ads) get a low multiple; downside is cliff-like (non-linear). "Volatility helps revenue ≠ helps the stock."
- **Consumer/retail** — spending *power* (PCE/GDP/tax policy) + spending *willingness* (consumer sentiment);
  mostly macro-driven + defensive.
- **Expectation gap** — when consensus misjudges a structural change, the gap itself is a bull driver;
  actively hunt "where is the common view wrong".
- **Second-order AI beneficiary + baton-pass** — some companies aren't AI players but AI's upstream
  necessity (a data platform: "all AI sits on data"); watch the old growth engine handing off to the new
  one; a platform can position itself as the "control plane".
- **Enumerate competition by category** (don't only name the biggest rival).

### ② Business model
- History → how it earns (revenue/customer mix) → name the *specific* moat type → durability → share/
  stickiness.
- **Moat library**: network effect (CUDA-style ecosystem) / switching cost + vertical depth + frontline
  engineers / scale + capital + first-mover ("winner-keeps-winning" foundry) / data & content flywheel /
  culture / one-stop platform / membership flywheel / **cross-cloud neutrality** ⚠️ for SaaS first name
  "the one thing rivals structurally can't do" (neutrality) *then* list switching costs — don't default to
  network effect.
- **Consumption (usage-based) pricing vs seats**: revenue scales with the customer's real usage → key
  metric **NRR (>120% healthy)**; double-edged — a macro chill / "usage optimization" cuts revenue faster
  than seat subscriptions.
- **Split the growth source (AI vs non-AI / by segment)** to see the real driver and expose single-driver/
  customer-concentration risk.
- **value-driver ≠ revenue-driver** — focus on what drives value, which may not be the revenue bulk.
- Vision-driven (pre-revenue): tell the long-term vision first, then how it earns today; state plainly that
  the future business is not yet monetized.

### ③ Management
- Value **technical background + long tenure + stable culture** (need not be a founder).
- **Critically appraise** — a professional manager isn't automatically positive; execution/bureaucracy gets
  called out; management can be a "question mark" or a net risk. But credit "dares to transform, cuts losses
  fast".
- **Judge professional (non-founder) CEOs by track record, not a mechanical penalty** — look at the actual
  transformation execution (a sales-led → product-led gene switch validated by results; "risk removed, trust
  restored" = net positive). A legendary predecessor leaving is a quiet loss, but validate via the successor's
  record, not a default markdown.
- **Founder-led = a plus** (higher ceiling); check dual-class control. **Double-edged**: concentrated control →
  over-reliance is a tail risk (→ ⑦).
- **Turnaround → the new leader is the thesis** — execution + prior-company track record is the core variable.
- Negative tilt: short-seller "great marketer, profits lag" claims, prior baggage, spread too thin.

### ④ Financials (quantitative core)
- **Growth** (front-load for growth/loss names): revenue YoY + *accelerating?* + by-segment + renewal/NRR.
- **Profitability**: **gross margin = most important, "higher = deeper moat"** — level + trend + benchmark
  (peer or SPX avg ~32%); ↑ = demand>supply (positive), ↓ = slowing/discounting (negative). ⚠️ **Exception**:
  membership/loss-leader retail deliberately runs low gross margin → "GM=moat" fails → use net-margin
  stability + renewal 90%+ + same-store + inventory turns. Net margin: level + path to profit.
- **Financial health**: **Debt / Total Assets < 40% (Buffett line; use TOTAL debt incl. capital leases — matches the ~20% read on AMZN; bond-only understates lease-heavy names; NOT D/E)** + FCF/operating CF
  persistently positive + heavy-asset → capex covered. 🔴 breaching 40% + chronic negative FCF = "unsafe /
  can't self-fund / high risk". Distinguish one-off vs structural negative FCF. **FCF yield** = cash-flow
  quality; cross-check FCF strength across big-tech to judge capex sustainability.
- **Capital-intensive / AI-infra / credit-sensitive** (3 extra): capex/revenue (extreme = a flag); net
  leverage = net debt/EBITDA (>3 downgrade risk / ~4 speculative); FCF turning negative on capex → watch
  rating agencies.
- **Bank/broker** (gross margin / debt ratio / FCF do NOT apply): efficiency ratio = opex/revenue (lower
  better) + ROTCE (drives the multiple) + CET1 (capital buffer).
- **High-growth SaaS / GAAP-loss**: don't flee the GAAP loss — **strip SBC first** (non-cash; add back to
  see real profit; check buyback offset → controllable) → judge profitability by **FCF margin (>20%)**;
  cash flow is strong because of (SBC add-back + usage contracts prepaid + asset-light low capex); net cash
  on a converts-vs-cash basis. SaaS gross margin is naturally stable → NOT a supply/demand thermometer.
- **Turnaround / heavy-asset IDM**: read the repair *slope/inflection*, not the absolute level; earnings
  swing harder.
- ⚠️ Compare margins like-for-like (don't pit gross margin vs operating margin). Over-high margins are
  fragile → also a ⑦ risk.
- Data: adapter `growth.*`, `quality.*`, `statements.debt_to_assets_pct (incl. leases) / gross_margin_trend_q /
  sbc_pct_revenue`, `valuation.ev_to_sales`.

### ⑤ Valuation — method by archetype (see the decision tree in 06-archetype-router.md)
- **Relative**: profitable large-cap → **forward PE (never trailing)**, triangulate SPX/own history/peers.
  Profitable high-growth software → high forward PE + P/S. **GAAP-loss / consumption SaaS → EV/Sales
  (cleaner than P/S; revenue is the least-distorted line; EV = price to buy the whole business; use the
  company's own multi-year EV/Sales history)** + **Meritech rule** (sustaining >10x needs growth >20% AND
  FCF margin >20%) + **quality-adjusted peer comp (not a single rival)**. Story/option stock → no relative
  multiples.
- **Bank → P/B × BPS** (ROTCE-driven). **Turnaround/trough → market-cap headroom** (TAM × share × net
  margin × PE + option value), because DCF + historical PE both break.
- **Absolute DCF**: base/bull/bear vs price. Story stocks → **SOTP-DCF** (discount each line; see where
  value concentrates).
- Explain *why* a multiple is structurally high/low (cyclicality + complexity → structural discount → can be
  "neither cheap nor expensive"). Vet whether "peers" are truly comparable. Target = your EPS × your
  multiple (start from consensus, adjust each leg with reasons).
- **Universal discipline**: ① base/bull/bear; ② **DCF base BELOW analyst mean**; ③ **target ∈ [base, bull]**
  (≠ simply base); ④ range conclusion (under/fair/over/"neither cheap nor expensive").
- ⭐ **Valuation ⊥ risk**: ⑤ = cheap-or-not, ⑦ = dare-to-buy. Can be "somewhat cheap" *and* "high risk".
  **"Expensive" can be the #1 risk** (slow to digest + no downside protection).

### ⑥ Investment logic (why buy)
Synthesize ①–⑤ → one-line bull + precondition. First **falsify the popular bear narrative with data**.
Archetypes: growth + moat → certainty / "toll-booth" independent of who wins downstream / mature compounder
(Costco-style: stickiness + price hikes + buybacks) / defensive counter-cyclical compounder / cyclical
recovery + structural improvement / turnaround (new CEO + catalyst + sector beta + expectation gap) / high
ceiling + firm floor. Story stocks → conditional bull. Soft logic: investor management (shareholder-letter
signals); ability to steer results = a kind of certainty. Mantra: **certainty > upside**.

### ⑦ Investment risk (why not buy)
Mantra: **the reason for success, flipped, is the biggest risk** — find the single most important metric, ask
when it deteriorates. Library: ① core driver disappoints (supplier-type → watch downstream capex) ②
competition/share/disruption ③ supply-chain/geopolitics/single-point ④ high-multiple growth → multiple
compression ⑤ founder/strong-man + key-man ⑥ heavy-asset depreciation lag ⑦ macro/cyclical demand ⑧
regulation/antitrust/privacy ⑨ capital misallocation/overspend ⑩ subsidy roll-off ⑪ story-stock tech never
ships ⑫ customer concentration ⑬ balance-sheet/capex spiral ⑭ strong-cycle downturn (non-linear) ⑮ systemic
(private credit) ⑯ valuation itself too high ⑰ slow innovation/AI adoption ⑱ post-rally pullback / yield ramp
/ can the turnaround become systemic ⑲ **business model bypassed by a new paradigm — ⑦ must always include
this layer**. ⚠️ Don't pile on risks the company has already neutralized (SBC offset by buyback = controllable);
contextualize, don't catastrophize.

## Aggregation + productized rating (see `references/05-rating-system.md`)
- **Two layers + coverage gate**: the free 5+2 = understanding layer (ends at "fairly valued + range", "not a
  buy/sell signal"); a paid/tracking product = conclusion layer (rating + tier + target + bear–bull range).
  **Only emit a rating card for a covered name.** Uncovered → understanding layer only.
- **Rating** ≈ upside-to-target × conviction (STRONG BUY ≥~28% + high conviction / BUY moderate-or-suppressed
  / HOLD limited or unproven). **Tier 1–4** = risk/certainty (1 safest, 4 most speculative), set by ⑦ + moat +
  balance sheet. **Target ∈ [DCF base, bull]**; range = DCF bear/bull.
